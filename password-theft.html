Password Thefts & Account Access

Understanding how attackers steal passwords and gain unauthorized access to accounts, and implementing effective protection measures

Overview

Password theft continues to be one of the most common and effective methods attackers use to gain unauthorized access to accounts and systems. In this module, you'll learn about different password theft techniques, how attackers use stolen credentials, and most importantly, how to protect yourself from these threats.

Beginner

Password Basics & Common Vulnerabilities

Why Passwords Matter

Passwords serve as the primary authentication method for most digital accounts. They are the first line of defense against unauthorized access. When a password is compromised, attackers can:

  • Access sensitive personal information
  • Make purchases or transfer funds
  • Steal your identity
  • Access your other accounts through password reuse
  • Use your accounts to attack others

Common Password Vulnerabilities

Most password vulnerabilities stem from user practices rather than technical flaws:

  • Weak Passwords: Short, simple, or predictable passwords that can be easily guessed
  • Password Reuse: Using the same password across multiple accounts
  • Infrequent Updates: Keeping the same passwords for extended periods
  • Predictable Patterns: Using obvious patterns like "Password123" or "Summer2023!"
  • Personal Information: Including personal details in passwords that could be guessed based on public information
Protection Measures
  • Strong, Unique Passwords: Use complex passwords with a mix of characters, numbers, and symbols. Create different passwords for different accounts.
  • Password Managers: Use a reputable password manager to generate and store strong, unique passwords for all your accounts.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Enable MFA wherever possible to add an additional layer of security beyond passwords.
  • Regular Updates: Change passwords periodically, especially for critical accounts.
  • Check for Breaches: Regularly check if your accounts have been compromised using services like Have I Been Pwned.

    Credential Stuffing Attacks

    What is Credential Stuffing?

    Credential stuffing is a cyberattack method where attackers use automated tools to inject stolen username and password pairs into website login forms, attempting to gain unauthorized access to user accounts.

    This attack works because many people reuse the same passwords across multiple sites. Once credentials are breached from one site, attackers can gain access to the victim's accounts on other sites.

    How Credential Stuffing Works

    A typical credential stuffing attack follows these steps:

    1. Data Collection: Attackers obtain databases of username/password combinations from data breaches or purchase them on dark web marketplaces.
    2. Automation Setup: They configure automated tools and bots to test these credentials against multiple websites.
    3. Mass Login Attempts: The bots attempt to log in to accounts across various websites with the stolen credentials.
    4. Account Takeover: When successful logins occur, attackers can take over accounts, steal information, make purchases, or conduct further attacks.

    These attacks are particularly dangerous because they're difficult to detect - each login attempt looks like a legitimate login from the correct username with the correct password.

    Real-World Impact

    Credential stuffing attacks have affected millions of accounts across various services:

    • In 2019, Disney+ experienced thousands of account compromises shortly after launch
    • Zoom faced credential stuffing attacks in 2020 where over 500,000 accounts were compromised
    • Streaming services like Netflix and Spotify regularly deal with credential stuffing attempts
    • Financial institutions are prime targets, with attackers seeking access to banking and payment systems
    Protection Measures
  • Use Unique Passwords: The primary defense is using different passwords for each account, which stops the cascade effect if one site is compromised.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Enabling MFA prevents account access even when credentials are compromised, as attackers won't have access to the secondary verification method.
  • Password Managers: Use a password manager to generate and store unique, complex passwords for all your accounts.
  • Monitor Account Activity: Regularly check for unauthorized access or suspicious activity on your accounts, especially after major data breaches.
  • Use Breach Notification Services: Services like "Have I Been Pwned" can alert you if your email appears in known data breaches.

    Password Spraying

    What is Password Spraying?

    Password spraying is a type of brute force attack where attackers attempt to access a large number of accounts using a small set of commonly used passwords. Unlike traditional brute force attacks that try many passwords against a single account (which can trigger lockouts), password spraying tries a single password against many accounts before moving to another password.

    This technique helps attackers avoid account lockouts while maximizing their chances of finding valid credentials.

    How Password Spraying Works

    A typical password spraying attack follows these steps:

    1. Target Identification: Attackers identify a target system or service with many users (corporate email, cloud services, etc.)
    2. Username Harvesting: They gather a list of valid usernames through techniques like OSINT (Open Source Intelligence), social media, or company directories
    3. Password Selection: They select a small list of commonly used passwords (e.g., "Password123", "Company2023", "Welcome1")
    4. Spraying: They attempt to login with the first password against all usernames, then wait before trying the second password
    5. Success Exploitation: Any successful logins are recorded and exploited for further access

    By spacing out the login attempts across accounts and timing, attackers can avoid triggering account lockout policies that would normally stop traditional brute force attacks.

    Common Targets and Risks

    Password spraying is particularly effective against:

    • Cloud Services: Microsoft 365, G Suite, AWS, and other cloud platforms
    • VPN Services: Remote access gateways that provide access to internal networks
    • SSO Systems: Single Sign-On systems that provide access to multiple applications
    • Enterprise Email: Corporate email systems that may have weak password policies

    The risk is magnified in organizations with weak password policies or where employees use predictable password patterns like "CompanyName2023" or "Summer2023!".

    Protection Measures
  • Strong Password Policies: Implement and enforce policies requiring complex passwords that aren't based on simple patterns or common words.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA is the most effective defense against password spraying as it requires an additional verification method beyond the password.
  • Account Lockout Policies: Configure systems to temporarily lock accounts after a certain number of failed attempts, while balancing security and usability.
  • Login Attempt Monitoring: Implement systems that can detect and alert on unusual login patterns across multiple accounts.
  • Password Breach Detection: Use tools that check passwords against databases of known breached passwords and prevent their use.

    Keyloggers & Malware

    Understanding Keyloggers

    Keyloggers are malicious software or hardware devices designed to record keystrokes made by users. They capture everything a user types, including usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, and personal messages.

    These tools operate silently in the background, with users typically unaware that their keystrokes are being recorded and transmitted to attackers.

    Types of Keyloggers

    Software Keyloggers

    • Kernel-based keyloggers: Operate at the deepest level of the operating system, intercepting keystrokes directly from the keyboard driver
    • API-based keyloggers: Intercept keyboard events through application programming interfaces
    • Form grabbers: Capture data specifically from web forms, often bypassing encryption
    • Browser-based keyloggers: JavaScript-based trackers injected into compromised websites

    Hardware Keyloggers

    • Physical keyboard devices: Small devices inserted between the keyboard and computer
    • Modified keyboards: Regular keyboards with keystroke recording technology embedded inside
    • Wireless sniffers: Capture data transmitted by wireless keyboards
    • Electromagnetic sniffers: Advanced devices that detect electromagnetic emissions from keyboards

    How Keyloggers Are Distributed

    Keyloggers typically reach victim computers through:

    • Phishing emails: Malicious attachments or links that install keyloggers when opened
    • Drive-by downloads: Automatic downloads from compromised or malicious websites
    • Bundled software: Hidden inside seemingly legitimate software packages
    • Physical access: Direct installation by someone with physical access to the device
    • Social engineering: Tricking users into installing malware by presenting it as useful software

    Other Password-Stealing Malware

    Beyond keyloggers, attackers use various types of malware to steal passwords:

    • Password stealers: Specialized malware that extracts saved passwords from browsers and applications
    • Screen scrapers: Capture screenshots when users interact with password fields or banking sites
    • Memory scrapers: Extract sensitive data directly from a computer's RAM
    • Man-in-the-Browser (MitB) attacks: Intercept data before encryption or after decryption within the browser
    • Clipboard hijackers: Monitor and steal data from the clipboard, targeting copied passwords
    Protection Measures
  • Keep Systems Updated: Apply security patches and updates for your operating system and applications promptly.
  • Use Anti-Malware Software: Install reputable security software that includes anti-keylogger protection.
  • Password Managers: Use password managers with auto-fill capabilities, which can be more resistant to keyloggers than manual typing.
  • Virtual Keyboards: For sensitive accounts, consider using on-screen virtual keyboards when entering passwords.
  • Two-Factor Authentication: Enable 2FA/MFA, so even if passwords are captured, attackers still can't access accounts.
  • Regular Scans: Perform regular malware scans of your system.
  • Physical Security: Be aware of your physical surroundings and check for unusual devices attached to your computer.

    Social Engineering Attacks

    What is Social Engineering?

    Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. Unlike technical hacking methods, social engineering exploits human psychology rather than software vulnerabilities.

    These attacks are particularly dangerous because they bypass technical security measures by targeting the most vulnerable element of any security system: the human user.

    Password-Related Social Engineering Tactics

    1. Phishing

    Attackers send fake emails, messages, or create counterfeit websites mimicking legitimate services to trick users into entering their credentials.

    • Spear phishing: Targeted attacks customized for specific individuals
    • Clone phishing: Replicating legitimate communications with malicious links
    • Vishing: Voice phishing over phone calls
    • Smishing: SMS phishing via text messages

    2. Pretexting

    Creating a fabricated scenario to extract information from victims, often by impersonating trusted entities such as:

    • IT support staff requesting passwords for "troubleshooting"
    • Company executives requiring urgent access
    • Financial institutions needing to "verify" account details

    3. Baiting

    Offering something enticing to victims in exchange for information or login actions:

    • Free software downloads that request account login
    • USB drives loaded with malware
    • "Free account upgrades" requiring verification

    4. Quid Pro Quo

    Promising a benefit in exchange for information, such as:

    • Fake tech support offering "assistance" in exchange for login credentials
    • Survey takers offering rewards for account verification

    Real-World Social Engineering Examples

    • Twitter VIP Hack (2020): Attackers used phone-based social engineering to convince Twitter employees to provide access to internal tools, leading to compromised high-profile accounts.
    • RSA Security Breach (2011): Employees were targeted with phishing emails containing Excel spreadsheets with embedded malware, leading to compromise of RSA's SecurID two-factor authentication system.
    • COVID-19 Themed Attacks: Widespread phishing campaigns using pandemic-related themes to steal credentials from users seeking information about vaccines, relief payments, and health updates.
    Protection Measures
  • Verification Protocols: Establish strict procedures for verifying identities before sharing sensitive information or credentials.
  • Security Awareness Training: Regular training to help employees and users recognize and respond to social engineering attempts.
  • Implement Authentication Rules: Create policies that prohibit password sharing under any circumstances, even with IT staff.
  • Verify Through Official Channels: Always confirm requests for sensitive information through official, previously established channels.
  • Healthy Skepticism: Develop a questioning mindset about unusual requests, especially those creating urgency or fear.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication: Implement MFA to mitigate damage from successful social engineering attacks.
  • Report Suspicious Activity: Establish clear procedures for reporting suspicious communications or activity.

    B Installing firewalls and antivirus software

    Incorrect. While technical measures like complex passwords and security software are important, social engineering bypasses these by exploiting human behavior. Security awareness training directly addresses this vulnerability by teaching people to recognize manipulation tactics.

    Protect Your Digital Identity

    Understanding password theft techniques is essential for protecting your digital identity in today's connected world. By implementing strong password practices, using multi-factor authentication, and staying vigilant against social engineering attempts, you can significantly reduce the risk of unauthorized access to your accounts.

    Remember: Your password security is only as strong as your weakest password. Take time to review and strengthen all your account credentials.
